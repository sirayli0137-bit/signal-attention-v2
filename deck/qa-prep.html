Signal — Q&A prep sheet

Pairs with the run sheet. Keep answers to ~30 seconds; end each on a design principle, not a feature.

The theme under every answer: AI accelerated everything; it decided nothing at the gates. I did.

Two “where I overrode the AI” anecdotes — have these ready verbatim

1 · The auto-resolve refusal. During ideation the AI scored “auto-resolve security alerts under a confidence threshold” as a top idea — the efficiency numbers looked great. I killed it. A false auto-resolve on a privilege escalation isn’t a UX miss; it’s a breach with a paper trail that says the system hid it. So Security is locked out of auto-handling by design — AI diagnoses and drafts the fix, a human clicks it. That single decision shaped the tier model, the activity log, and the copilot’s guardrails.
2 · Auditing the auditor. I ran AI design-critique skills against my own AI-built prototype — 17 findings. But I didn’t apply them wholesale: I triaged all 17 myself, accepted the contrast and honesty fixes (secondary buttons were silently dismissing alerts — a trust bug the AI caught), and rejected the suggestions that traded Mara’s calm for feature density. The audit is on the canvas: findings, my verdicts, and the diff between v1 and v1.3.

Ten anticipated questions

1Why an Admin Center and not Xero’s accounting product?
Deliberate scope call. Notifications are the brief; accounting depth isn’t — and I’d lose to your own domain experts pretending otherwise. An admin console gives genuinely high-stakes, high-noise notifications (security, billing, access) so we can argue design, not domain. The system — rank by consequence, deliver by context — ports straight onto Xero’s surfaces.
2How does the scoring actually work?
Today: typed rules — severity × impact (who/what it touches: money, access, data) × time pressure → three tiers. That’s honest v1; it’s deterministic and explainable. The model earns its way in later, per event-type, only where its precision beats the rules — and every score keeps a “why now” explanation attached, because an unexplained rank is just a new kind of noise.
3What happens when the AI gets it wrong?
Three nets: wrong tier → any surface lets you escalate or mute in one tap, and that correction feeds routing; wrong auto-action → everything auto-handled is logged and undoable; wrong on security → impossible by design, it never auto-acts there. The failure mode we optimise against is the silent one.
4Isn’t this just PagerDuty / Opsgenie?
Those route incidents to on-call engineers who chose severity ladders as a job. Mara is an owner-admin with no on-call rota and no config appetite. Signal’s bet is the inverse: smart defaults with light override, auto-handling of the routine, and a digest — attention management for people who won’t set up escalation policies, which is most admins.
5How does this scale to enterprise?
Same spine, more governance: routing rules become policies (who may auto-handle what), the activity log becomes an audit export, tiers map to team queues instead of one person. Dev the IT admin is already in the archetypes — enterprise gets power on top of Mara’s defaults, not a different product.
6Doesn’t the digest just bury things?
Only what the system has already handled or ranked as safe-to-wait — criticals always break through, even at 20:30. And the digest isn’t a dump: it leads with a recommendation you can act on in one tap. The risk to manage is misclassification, which is question 3 — escalate-in-one-tap is the net.
7What would you validate first with real users?
The trust gate: do admins let “Handled for you” stay handled, or do they re-check everything? Instrument re-open rate on auto-handled items and undo rate. If those don’t fall over the first weeks, the whole premise — attention returned, not just re-sorted — fails, and no visual polish rescues it.
8How did you actually use AI in this project?
A master prompt with six human checkpoints: AI diverged (18 ideas), swept 26 competitor patterns, generated boards and the prototype, audited my prototype (17 findings) — and at every gate I chose direction, killed ideas, re-threaded the narrative. The two stories I’d point to: the auto-resolve refusal and auditing-the-auditor (above).
9Why three tiers and not priorities P1–P4 or a smart-sorted single feed?
Tiers answer Mara’s actual question — “do I need to do anything?” — not “how important is this abstractly”. P-levels make her rank; a smart-sorted stream still makes her read everything. Three tiers map to three behaviours: act now, glance later, ignore safely. The third one is the product.
10What’s the business case?
Two lines: risk — missed criticals are churn-grade events (a breach or a failed payroll run costs the account), and Signal cuts time-to-action on those to 90 seconds; cost — admin attention is the scarcest seat in an SME, and ↓62% interruptions is retention for the whole workspace, not a nicety. Trust metrics (undo rate, re-open rate) are the leading indicators.
