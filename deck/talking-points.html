Talking points — slide by slide

Casual version — this is roughly what you say out loud, not what's on the screen. Pairs with the run sheet (timing) and Q&A prep. Italics = say it close to verbatim.

If you only memorise one line: "Rank by consequence. Deliver by context. Earn trust by showing your work." Open with it, close with it.

1Title

  • "Hi — I'm going to show you Signal. It's an attention layer for admins, and the whole idea fits in one sentence: rank by consequence, deliver by context, earn trust by showing your work."
  • "Everything for the next twenty minutes is just that sentence, unpacked."

2How I ran it

  • "Quick word on process before the work, because the brief asked me to use AI — and I did, a lot. But with structure."
  • "I wrote one master prompt with six checkpoints. The AI built everything between the gates — research sweeps, boards, even the prototype. At each gate, it stopped and I chose the direction."
  • Then just read the scoreboard casually: "So: 6 checkpoints, 18 ideas explored, 26 competitor patterns, 17 audit findings, 3 prototype versions. I'll flag the moments where I overrode it as we go."

3The frame

  • "First decision: where to set this. I picked an admin center — think Atlassian admin or Google Workspace admin — not an accounting product."
  • "Two reasons. Admin notifications are genuinely high-stakes and genuinely noisy — security, billing, access. And honestly, I wasn't going to out-domain your accountants in a week. This way we argue about design, not about tax rules."
  • If it feels needed: "Everything here ports straight onto Xero's surfaces — the setting is a stand-in, the system isn't."

4Archetypes

  • "Meet Mara. She owns a 40-person studio, she's the admin because someone had to be, and she will never — ever — open a notification settings page."
  • "She's the design target. If she can trust this hands-off, then the IT admin and the enterprise team get power on top of her defaults — not a separate product."

5HEART — how we'd measure it

  • "Success metrics up front, so the design has something to answer to."
  • "The unusual one: Retention here means how much she lets the AI carry — does 'handled for you' stay handled, or does she re-check everything? I call it the letting-go metric."
  • "And trust and transparency isn't a metric alongside the others — it's the gate. If that fails, nothing else matters."

6Her week — the insight slide

  • Slow down here; this is the "aha". "We mapped her actual week, and here's the thing: about 90% of it is spent away from the desk. On site, in meetings, at home."
  • "Which means the interesting design question isn't what the notification says — it's where and when it's allowed to reach her. The channel is the design."
  • "So: critical breaks through anywhere, even by SMS. Action items get one well-timed push. Routine gets handled or batched into an evening digest. And deep triage waits for the desk."

7Three problems

  • "That boils down to three problems to solve: reach her at the right moment in the right channel, let her act without any setup homework, and earn enough trust that she can actually let go."
  • "One system answers all three — that's what the rest of this is."

8Explored wide

  • Don't read this slide — gesture at it. "We went wide first: 18 ideas, from tiny tweaks to full agentic copilots, each one traced to a real product doing it today."
  • "The point of the width was to make the narrowing honest."

9Scored & narrowed

  • "Scored on impact, effort and how well it scales. Two winners for now: the triage brain — deciding what matters — and the router — deciding where it goes."
  • "The copilot scored well too, and I deliberately pushed it later. An assistant you don't trust yet is just another notification. It has to earn its way in." (This is override story #1 territory if anyone asks.)

10The flow

  • "The end-to-end flow isn't copied from any one competitor — it's a synthesis, and every piece is cited: PagerDuty's routing spine, iOS's digest-by-default, Gmail's single feed."
  • "Steal the best pattern for each job, and make them agree with each other."

11System × channels

  • "This grid is the whole product on one slide. Rows are the system — the eight layers. Columns are delivery — app, desktop, SMS, Slack."
  • Physically point at the empty cells: "And look at what's empty. Most of this grid is deliberately silent. The calm isn't an absence of design — it is the design."

12The stack

  • "Each layer's final pick, each one tied back to a principle — nothing in here is a vibe."
  • Then the pivot, with energy: "That's the theory. Let me show you the real thing." → switch tabs.

13DEMO (the 4-minute centrepiece)

  • Narrate what Mara feels, not what you click. Rough script:
  • "This is her Monday inbox the old way — 16 notifications, all equal weight. Two of them actually matter. Find them." (pause a beat) "Exactly." Then flip the toggle: "Same morning, with Signal: three need her, 118 handled without her."
  • Act on the security item: "One click to revoke — and undo is right there. Nothing is a leap of faith."
  • Log: "Everything the AI did on its own is here, in plain language, reversible. This page is why she can let go."
  • Digest tab: "The weekly picture, plus one recommendation she can apply in a tap — and it logs itself."
  • Ask Signal: "And when she has a question, she just asks — the answer links her straight to the item."
  • Mobile: "Out of the office at 10:02, only the critical breaks through — trend chart, one confirm, done in 90 seconds. Then nothing until 20:30: one digest ping. Nothing urgent — sleep well. That's the product."

14–24Backup screenshots

  • Only if the live demo dies. Same script as above, one screenshot per beat. Press B to skip past them when the demo worked.

25Audited & refined

  • "I didn't stop at version one. I turned the AI design-critique tools and our competitive research on my own prototype — 17 findings."
  • "Best catch: my secondary buttons were quietly dismissing alerts while pretending to do something. A trust bug in a product about trust. Fixed, along with contrast, touch targets, a working undo. Three versions in, same URL."

26AI in my process

  • "So, where did AI help and where did I overrule it? Short version: it accelerated everything and decided nothing at the gates."
  • Tell story 1: "It scored 'auto-resolve security alerts' as a top idea — great efficiency numbers. I killed it, because a false auto-resolve on a privilege escalation isn't a UX miss, it's a hidden breach. That one call shaped the tiers, the log, the guardrails."
  • Tease story 2: "And even the audit — I triaged all 17 findings myself and rejected the ones that traded Mara's calm for density. Happy to go deeper in Q&A."

27If we had more time

  • "What's next is deliberately unglamorous: put this in front of real admins before adding more polish, swap the typed rules for a model behind the same interface, and measure the trust gate — do people actually let go?"

28Close

  • Bring it home, slowly: "So — rank by consequence. Deliver by context. Earn trust by showing your work. The prototype's live at the link, the whole process is on the canvas, and I'd love your questions."
