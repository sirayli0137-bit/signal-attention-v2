Talking points — slide by slide

Casual version — this is roughly what you say out loud, not what's on the screen. Pairs with the run sheet (timing) and Q&A prep. Italics = say it close to verbatim. boards = the MagicPath board the point comes from, so you can pull evidence in Q&A.

If you only memorise one line: "Rank by consequence. Deliver by context. Earn trust by showing your work." Open with it, close with it.

1Title

  • "Hi — I'm going to show you Signal. It's an attention layer for admins, and the whole idea fits in one sentence: rank by consequence, deliver by context, earn trust by showing your work."
  • "Everything for the next twenty minutes is just that sentence, unpacked."

2How I ran it — four AI practices

  • "Quick word on process, because the brief asked me to use AI — and I did, heavily. But with structure. Four practices working together."
  • MASS prompt — the spine: "The core is what I call the MASS prompt — a methodology for using AI to broaden and deepen each divergent and convergent phase, with a human in the loop bringing the critical, creative judgement. One master prompt, six checkpoints. The AI built everything between the gates; at each gate it stopped, and I chose the direction."
  • Context engineering: "Before anything ran, I put the design system and the brief overview in place at the project level — so every single output starts on-brand and on-brief instead of generic. On bigger projects that context can sit even higher — problem-space level, or global."
  • Prompt engineering: "At each step I fed it as much context as I could, mostly by voice — talking is much higher bandwidth than typing, so the AI got the full picture every time."
  • Automated research & synthesis: "And I chained design-critique skills, MCP integrations like Mobbin, screenshot references and auto-validation so comparative research and quality checks ran automatically — keeping the output up to standard and aligned with where I was steering."
  • Close with the scoreboard, one breath: "Net result: 6 checkpoints, 18 ideas, 26 cited patterns, 17 audit findings, 3 prototype versions. I'll flag the moments where I overrode the AI as we go."

3The frame

  • "First decision: where to set this. I picked an admin center — think Atlassian admin or Google Workspace admin — not an accounting product."
  • "Two reasons. Admin notifications are genuinely high-stakes and genuinely noisy — security, billing, access. And honestly, I wasn't going to out-domain your accountants in a week. This way we argue about design, not about tax rules."
  • If it feels needed: "Everything here ports straight onto Xero's surfaces — the setting is a stand-in, the system isn't."

4Archetypes

  • "Meet Mara. She owns a 40-person studio, she's the admin because someone had to be, and she will never — ever — open a notification settings page."
  • "She's the design target. If she can trust this hands-off, then the IT admin and the enterprise team get power on top of her defaults — not a separate product."

5HEART — how we'd measure it

  • "Success metrics up front, so the design has something to answer to."
  • "The unusual one: Retention here means how much she lets the AI carry — does 'handled for you' stay handled, or does she re-check everything? I call it the letting-go metric."
  • "And trust and transparency isn't a metric alongside the others — it's the gate. If that fails, nothing else matters."

6Her week — the insight slide

  • Slow down here; this is the "aha". "We mapped her actual week, and here's the thing: about 90% of it is spent away from the desk. On site, in meetings, at home."
  • "Which means the interesting design question isn't what the notification says — it's where and when it's allowed to reach her. The channel is the design."
  • "So: critical breaks through anywhere, even by SMS. Action items get one well-timed push. Routine gets handled or batched into an evening digest. And deep triage waits for the desk."

7Three problems

  • "That boils down to three problems to solve: reach her at the right moment in the right channel, let her act without any setup homework, and earn enough trust that she can actually let go."

8Five design principles board 13

  • "Before any ideas, I turned her problems into five principles — these are the judges for everything that follows."
  • Walk them fast, one line each: "Minimal disruption — only what truly needs her breaks through; everything else waits. Effortless efficiency — understand and act in one tap, zero setup. Right moment, right channel — reach her by severity and context, not by the clock. Meaningful control — see, adjust, reverse; control that builds trust, not config work. And transparency earns confidence — always show what the AI did and why."
  • The shape of it: "Notice the tension — minimal disruption and effortless efficiency pull against each other. Meaningful control is what holds them in balance, and transparency is how it earns the right to."

9Explored wide ("Everything on the table")

  • Don't read this slide — gesture at it. "With the principles set, we went wide: 18 ideas, from tiny tweaks to full agentic copilots, each one traced to a real product doing it today."
  • "The point of the width was to make the narrowing honest."

10Scored & narrowed

  • "Scored on impact, effort and how well it scales. Two winners for now: the triage brain — deciding what matters — and the router — deciding where it goes."
  • "The copilot scored well too, and I deliberately pushed it later. An assistant you don't trust yet is just another notification. It has to earn its way in."

11The flow

  • "The end-to-end flow isn't copied from any one competitor — it's a synthesis, and every piece is cited: PagerDuty's routing spine, iOS's digest-by-default, Gmail's single feed."

12System × channels

  • "This grid is the whole product on one slide. Rows are the system — the eight layers. Columns are delivery — app, desktop, SMS, Slack."
  • Physically point at the empty cells: "And look at what's empty. Most of this grid is deliberately silent. The calm isn't an absence of design — it is the design."

13Mara's future journey — the layers to design board 10

  • "Here's her future journey, and it's also the map for the demo. An event fires while she's away — the AI scores and routes it. Critical? It breaks through, wherever she is. She decides and acts on one card — severity, impact, why, one tap. She gets confirmation — 'revoked, user notified', no guessing. Back at the desk she catches up on the triaged feed, checks the activity log to see what the AI did — that's the trust surface — and her day ends with one calm evening summary."
  • "Each step of that journey is a surface to design — seven now, plus the copilot later. And I'm about to demo it in exactly this order: break-through, native app, desktop, digest."

14The stack

  • "Each layer's final pick, each one tied back to a principle — nothing in here is a vibe."
  • Then the pivot, with energy: "That's the theory. Let me show you the real thing — in her order, through her day." → switch tabs.

15 · demoStage 1 — Break-through (10:02, on site) boards 39 · 19

  • Start in the mobile view. "It's 10:02, Mara's on site. An external guest just got made admin — the AI scores it critical, and it's the only thing allowed to reach her."
  • Channels point 39: "And notice it's not 'the app or nothing'. The same alert atom renders to whatever channel she actually has: SMS as plain text — reply 1 to revoke; Slack as a full interactive card with buttons; WhatsApp as quick-replies. The actions degrade gracefully with the channel — and email deliberately gets no live actions at all, it's only ever the digest."
  • Routing line 39: "Severity times context — plus whatever the org already runs — picks where it lands. The enterprise admin gets it through Slack or PagerDuty they already live in."
  • Depth-on-demand 19: "The push itself is progressive: glance — one line on the lock screen. Tap — the data: for billing that's the usage trend against the suspension threshold with a countdown; access gets a requests-per-week bar; security gets the sign-in story. Same pattern, per domain. Then confirm — one tap, card on file, no app, no forms."

15 · demoStage 2 — Native app (the rich card) boards 37 · 16 · 31

  • Tap into the card. "On the phone, the app plays two roles: a tripwire for criticals, and a calm catch-up for everything else — never a place she has to manage." 37
  • Walk the card 16: "Simple to glance, deep on tap. The AI's narrative in plain English, then the exact 'what changed' — who, granted by whom, what scope. Enough to decide with confidence."
  • "One tap to act, always reversible — or she can ask Signal a follow-up right here. And she can swipe to the next of her three." 16
  • Design rationale, worth saying out loud 31: "Design detail I care about: the card is quiet by design. Colour appears exactly twice — the severity dot, and the one action that matters. Red is reserved for destructive. Everything else is typography and space. Quiet card, loud signal."

15 · demoStage 3 — Desktop (the deep workbench) boards 38 · 15 · 40 · 17 · 18

  • Switch to desktop. "Back at her desk — this is the deep workbench. The whole stack lives here: triage, decide, confirm, trust, govern, ask." 38
  • Flip the before/after toggle first: "This is her Monday the old way — sixteen notifications, all equal weight. Two actually matter. Find them." (pause) "Exactly. Same morning with Signal: three need her, the rest handled or held."
  • Select the payroll backlog item 15: "The detail pane carries real depth: the metric — 11 of 12 requests expected, one flagged external contractor — the entity chips, the AI's 'why now' in plain English, and a recommended action: approve the eleven, hold the contractor for review. She's deciding, not reading."
  • Act on the security item, then undo: "One click to revoke — and undo is right there. Nothing is a leap of faith."
  • Activity log 17: "And this is the page that makes letting go safe: what Signal did and why. Routed the critical to her phone. Auto-approved two joins — known domain, her policy — undoable. Held twelve sign-ins for the digest. Deduped nine identical alerts into one. Every AI decision: visible, explained, reversible. This is the trust gate made concrete."
  • Controls drawer: "Control without homework — the AI set these defaults, she can nudge them, and Security is locked out of auto-handling by design."
  • Ask Signal 18: "And when she has a question she just asks — 'who has admin access I should review?' — and the answer deep-links straight to the flagged item. Behind it, the data view: 118 things handled without her in 30 days, interruptions down 62% — and a timeline where quiet days stay visibly quiet. We even show the 'nothing' days, because visible silence is what earns trust."
  • Honesty beat if there's air 40: "And this build is audited — I ran heuristics against it, found three P1s including secondary buttons that lied, and fixed them. More on that in a minute."

15 · demoStage 4 — Email / digest (20:30) board 33

  • Digest tab on desktop: "Last surface: the rhythm. One digest, two renderings. At the desk it's a review, not a list — the week at a glance, and a recommendation she can act on: reduce the access-request backlog by 71 percent by mapping the team to the SCIM group — that's 18 fewer manual requests a week. One tap to apply, and yes, it logs itself."
  • Switch to mobile, 20:30: "On the phone that same digest is a stat-glance: zero need you, six handled, seven worth knowing. Nothing needed you today."
  • The ask + close of the demo: "She can still ask — 'anything for tomorrow?' — nothing urgent. Sleep well. That's the product: her attention back, with receipts."

27Audited & refined board 40

  • "I didn't stop at version one. I turned the AI design-critique tools and our competitive research on my own prototype — 17 findings, heuristic score 24 out of 40 on the first pass."
  • "Best catch: my secondary buttons were quietly dismissing alerts while pretending to do something. A trust bug in a product about trust. Fixed, along with contrast, touch targets, a dead undo. Three versions in, same URL."

28AI in my process

  • "So, where did AI help and where did I overrule it? Short version: it accelerated everything and decided nothing at the gates."
  • Story 1: "It scored 'auto-resolve security alerts' as a top idea — great efficiency numbers. I killed it, because a false auto-resolve on a privilege escalation isn't a UX miss, it's a hidden breach. That one call shaped the tiers, the log, the guardrails."
  • Story 2 tease: "And even the audit — I triaged all 17 findings myself and rejected the ones that traded Mara's calm for density. Happy to go deeper in Q&A."
